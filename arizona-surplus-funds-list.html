What Are Arizona Surplus Funds?

When a property is foreclosed and sold at auction, the final sale price sometimes exceeds the amount owed on the mortgage or liens. When this happens, the remaining money becomes foreclosure surplus funds (also known as excess proceeds or foreclosure overages).

These funds are typically held by the court until the rightful party files a claim. In many cases the person entitled to the funds is the former homeowner or their heirs.

Unfortunately, many Arizona residents never realize they are entitled to this money. As a result, thousands of dollars in surplus funds remain unclaimed across the state each year.

These funds legally belong to the former property owner, heirs, or lienholders and are typically held by the county treasurer or the court until a claim is filed.

What Are Surplus Funds?

Surplus funds occur when a foreclosed property sells for more than the mortgage balance, taxes, and foreclosure costs. The remaining money must be deposited with the county treasurer or court for distribution to the rightful owner.

In Arizona, the process is governed by statutes such as A.R.S. §33-812 which regulates how excess proceeds from trustee sales are distributed.

Many property owners are never aware this money exists, which is why millions of dollars remain unclaimed each year.

How Surplus Funds Are Created

A surplus occurs when a property sells for more than the total debt owed. For example:

  • Mortgage owed: $200,000
  • Foreclosure sale price: $260,000
  • Costs and liens: $20,000
  • Remaining surplus: $40,000

The remaining funds are deposited with the county treasurer or court until someone files a legal claim.
