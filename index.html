What We Recover

Types of Unclaimed Funds We Recover

Foreclosure Surplus Funds

When a lender forecloses and the auction price exceeds the mortgage balance, the excess is owed to the former homeowner. These foreclosure overages are held by county courts.

Tax Sale Excess Proceeds

When a county sells property for unpaid taxes and the sale generates more than the debt, the tax sale surplus belongs to the former owner.

Bank Accounts, Stock & Court Funds

Dormant bank accounts, uncashed checks, stock dividends, court deposits, escrow accounts — billions sit abandoned in state treasuries every year.

Insurance, Trusts & More

· Insurance Payouts · Trust Distributions · Refunds · Annuities · Contents of Safe Deposit Boxes · Utility Security Payments · Mineral Royalty Payments · Refunds on Life Insurance Policies

Why Most Funds Go Unclaimed

Government agencies are not in the business of tracking you down to give you money. If no one files a claim, the funds often end up absorbed into county and state budgets. Without professional help, claimants can miss deadlines, fail to meet documentation requirements, or simply never hear about the funds in the first place.

Our mission is to bridge that gap — making sure people are informed and empowered to recover what's rightfully theirs. We believe this money is better in the hands of individuals, families, and businesses who it belongs to.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are surplus funds after a foreclosure? +
Surplus funds are money left over after a foreclosure or tax sale pays off all debts. The former property owner is legally entitled to claim these funds — but most people never know they exist.
How much does it cost? +
Absolutely no upfront cost. We work on contingency — we only earn a fee if we successfully recover money for you. Zero risk.
How long do I have to claim? +
Claim windows vary by state. Texas: 2 years. Georgia: up to 5 years. Arizona varies by sale type. Act quickly — deadlines are strictly enforced.
