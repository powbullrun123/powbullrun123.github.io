What Are Foreclosure Surplus Funds in Arizona?

In Arizona, most residential foreclosures are conducted through a non-judicial trustee sale process. When a foreclosed property is sold at auction for more than the total amount owed — including the mortgage balance, interest, penalties, and foreclosure costs — the remaining balance is called excess proceeds or surplus funds.

Under Arizona law (A.R.S. § 33-812), these excess proceeds may be claimed by the former property owner or other entitled parties in order of legal priority. However, this money is never automatically returned to you — a formal claim must be filed with the appropriate trustee or court to recover it.

These funds are commonly referred to as:

  • Foreclosure surplus funds
  • Excess proceeds from trustee sale
  • Foreclosure overages
  • Trustee sale surplus
  • Unclaimed foreclosure funds

Arizona Tax Lien Sales & Overages

Arizona counties also conduct tax lien sales when property taxes go unpaid. When a tax-liened property eventually sells at auction and the sale price exceeds the delinquent taxes, interest, penalties, and costs owed, the difference becomes tax sale surplus funds.

These overages are separate from foreclosure surplus funds but follow a similar claims process. In high-value counties like Maricopa and Pima, tax sale surpluses can be substantial — sometimes tens of thousands of dollars — and often go entirely unclaimed because the former owner had no idea the money existed.

⚠️ Arizona Deadlines Are Strict

Arizona law sets firm deadlines for filing surplus fund claims after a trustee sale. In many cases, once a statutory period passes, unclaimed funds are transferred to the state as unclaimed property — making recovery significantly harder and sometimes impossible. If you believe you may have a claim, act quickly.

Who Can Claim Arizona Surplus Funds?

The following parties may have a legal right to claim excess proceeds from an Arizona foreclosure or tax sale:

  • Former property owners — the primary claimant in most cases
  • Heirs of a deceased homeowner — including those navigating probate
  • Junior lienholders — second mortgage holders, HOA lien holders, etc.
  • Co-owners or divorced spouses — depending on ownership structure
  • Legal representatives — executors, trustees, or attorneys acting on behalf of the above

Even if you have moved out of state, gone through divorce, or are managing the estate of a deceased relative, you may still be eligible to file a claim. Our team can help you determine eligibility at no cost.
